## $3,591. That's It.

Out of 690 schools in our dataset, Berea College in Berea, Kentucky has the lowest median student debt: $3,591. The dataset median is $19,500. Berea's graduates leave with 82% less debt than the typical college student.

The debt-to-income ratio tells the rest of the story. At 0.08, Berea has the best ratio in the entire dataset — better than MIT (0.10), Johns Hopkins (0.12), and Wellesley (0.12). Those schools achieve low ratios through high earnings. Berea achieves it through near-zero debt.

## The Tuition-Free Model

Berea College admits only students who demonstrate financial need, then covers 100% of tuition for every admitted student. No merit scholarship lottery. No complex financial aid packages. Every student gets the same deal: full tuition, covered.

The result is a private, non-profit college where the median graduate owes less than the cost of a used car.

## The Numbers in Context

**Berea College** — Median debt: $3,591. Ten-year earnings: $43,150. D/I ratio: 0.08. ROI score: 53.

**Strayer University** — Median debt: $40,621. Ten-year earnings: $40,092. D/I ratio: 1.01. ROI score: 13.

Read those numbers again. Strayer graduates carry 11x more debt than Berea graduates and earn *less*. A Strayer graduate's entire first year of post-graduation earnings wouldn't cover their student loans. A Berea graduate could pay off their total debt in a single month.

That's the difference between a debt-to-income ratio of 0.08 and 1.01. It's not a marginal gap — it's a different financial universe.

## The Accessible Low-Debt Story

The other schools near the top of the debt-to-income rankings — MIT, Johns Hopkins, Wellesley — are among the most selective institutions in the country. Their low ratios come from graduates earning $85K–$143K a decade out. The admissions barrier is extreme.

Berea's path to low debt is fundamentally different. It doesn't require elite academic credentials or family connections. It requires financial need. The school exists specifically for students who can't afford college otherwise.

That distinction matters. MIT proves that elite education can produce exceptional returns. Berea proves that eliminating tuition produces exceptional debt outcomes for students who'd otherwise borrow their way through a degree — or not go at all.

## The Takeaway

$3,591 in median debt. A 0.08 debt-to-income ratio. The best in the dataset, produced by a school most families outside Kentucky have never heard of.

Berea College doesn't compete with MIT on earnings. It competes on a different axis entirely: what's the least amount of financial damage a college education can do? The answer, according to the data, is almost none.
