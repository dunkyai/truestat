## Cheap Doesn't Mean Valuable

The conventional wisdom is simple: go to community college, save money, come out ahead. And for most affordable schools, that math works. Among the 96 public colleges in our dataset charging under $10,000 per year, the median ROI score is 39 out of 100 — respectable for low-cost institutions.

But 9% of those schools score below 30. Their graduates earn $32,000–$37,000 a decade after enrollment — well below the $44,425 national median across all institutions. When even near-free tuition can't offset weak earnings, the problem isn't price. It's outcomes.

## The Schools

Here are six of the cheapest colleges in America with the poorest ROI scores in our analysis.

**Henry Ford College** (Dearborn, MI) — $660/yr in-state tuition. ROI score: 26. Graduates earn $34,795 at the 10-year mark. The graduation rate is 13.8%, meaning fewer than 1 in 7 students finish. At $660 a year, the tuition cost is almost irrelevant. The real cost is the time spent not finishing.

**Haskell Indian Nations University** (Lawrence, KS) — $3,134/yr. ROI score: 26. Ten-year earnings: $37,043. Graduation rate: 30.5%. A federally funded tribal university, Haskell serves a specific mission. But its graduates still earn $7,400 less than the national median a decade out.

**Chesapeake College** (Wye Mills, MD) — $5,106/yr. ROI score: 24. Ten-year earnings: $36,301. Graduation rate: 27.3%. A rural Eastern Shore community college where barely one in four students completes a credential.

**Washington County Community College** (Calais, ME) — $5,149/yr. ROI score: 26. Ten-year earnings: $34,407. Graduation rate: 48.5%. The highest completion rate on this list, yet graduates earn $10,000 below the national median. Location matters — Calais sits on the Canadian border in Maine's poorest county.

**SOWELA Technical Community College** (Lake Charles, LA) — $7,525/yr. ROI score: 24. Ten-year earnings: $32,303. Graduation rate: 43.0%. Despite the "technical" focus that typically boosts earnings, SOWELA graduates have the lowest 10-year earnings on this list.

**Kentucky State University** (Frankfort, KY) — $8,040/yr. ROI score: 18. Ten-year earnings: $36,382. Graduation rate: 29.5%. Median debt: $25,938. The worst ROI score on this list — and the only school here where graduates carry significant debt. An HBCU with a $26K debt load and $36K earnings creates a debt-to-income ratio of 0.71, well above the 0.5 threshold that signals repayment risk.

## What's Actually Driving Poor ROI

Tuition is one input. The real ROI equation has three other variables these schools struggle with:

**Graduation rates.** Four of these six schools graduate fewer than 31% of their students. Every non-completer still spent time and opportunity cost — often taking on some debt — without earning the credential that drives higher wages.

**Regional labor markets.** Calais, Maine. Lake Charles, Louisiana. Rural Maryland. These schools feed into local economies with limited high-wage employment. A degree's value is partly a function of where you can use it.

**Program mix.** Schools with heavy concentrations in liberal arts, general studies, and low-earning vocational tracks produce graduates whose credentials don't command wage premiums — regardless of what the tuition was.

## The Opportunity Cost Problem

A student at Henry Ford College pays $660 per year in tuition. Over two years, that's $1,320. Practically free.

But that student also spent two years not earning a full-time wage. If the alternative was a job paying $30,000 per year, the true cost of attendance isn't $1,320 — it's $61,320 in lost earnings plus tuition. And with a 13.8% graduation rate, there's an 86% chance that student walks away without the credential.

This is why ROI analysis matters more than sticker price. A $660 degree sounds like a deal. A two-year investment with an 86% failure rate and $35K earnings on the other side is not.

## The Takeaway

Most affordable colleges deliver solid value. But "affordable" is not a synonym for "worth it." Before choosing a school based on price alone, check three things: the graduation rate, the 10-year median earnings for graduates, and the local job market.

A school can cost almost nothing and still be a poor investment if it doesn't get students across the finish line — or if the finish line doesn't lead anywhere.
