## The Question Everyone Asks

Is college worth it? The answer, it turns out, depends enormously on where you go and what you study. A computer science degree from MIT and a liberal arts degree from a low-ranked private school are not the same investment — yet they're often discussed as if they are.

We built TrueStat to bring clarity to this question with data, not opinions.

## What We Analyzed

Our dataset covers **6,322 U.S. colleges and universities**, spanning every accredited institution that reports outcomes data to the federal government. For each school, we track tuition costs, graduation rates, median earnings at multiple time horizons, debt loads, and repayment rates.

We also break down outcomes by major. Across the dataset, we track **421 distinct fields of study**, from Aerospace Engineering to Zoology. Each major at each school gets its own ROI assessment based on actual graduate earnings versus the cost of attendance.

## The ROI Score

Every school receives a TrueStat ROI Score from 0 to 100. This composite metric weighs earnings potential against total cost, accounting for graduation probability, time to degree, and debt burden. It's designed to capture the full economic picture, not just starting salary.

At the top of our rankings, MIT leads with a score of **95 out of 100** — driven by exceptional mid-career earnings, high graduation rates, and strong outcomes across nearly every field of study. But you don't need to attend an elite institution to get a strong ROI. Many public universities and specialized technical schools score above 80.

## What You Can Find on TrueStat

- **School profiles** with detailed financial metrics, ROI scores, and major-level breakdowns
- **State-by-state comparisons** showing which regions offer the best value
- **Major analysis** revealing which fields of study consistently deliver strong returns
- **Head-to-head comparisons** so you can evaluate your specific options

## Our Approach

We believe in transparency. Every metric on TrueStat links back to its source data. We use federal datasets including the College Scorecard, IPEDS, and Bureau of Labor Statistics employment projections. Where we apply methodology — like our ROI scoring formula — we document it clearly.

The goal isn't to tell you where to go to school. It's to make sure you have the numbers you need to make that decision yourself.

## What's Next

We're actively expanding our analysis. Upcoming additions include time-series trends (how has a school's ROI changed over the past decade?), employer hiring data, and geographic earnings adjustments. If a degree pays well in San Francisco but you plan to live in Ohio, that context matters.
